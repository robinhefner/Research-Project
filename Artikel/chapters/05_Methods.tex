This section describes the methodological framework employed to evaluate synthetic clinical data generation and the integration of sensor data into the Heidelberg studiKIS. The research followed a structured four-phase approach.

\subsection*{Literature Review}
A comprehensive literature review was conducted to identify and analyze current state-of-the-art approaches, focusing on studies that addressed at least one of the following two primary domains:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Synthetic Clinical Data Generation:} Investigation of Large Language Models (LLMs) and autonomous agent systems, encompassing both classical rule-based architectures and modern LLM-based agents for the creation of longitudinal patient records.
\item \textbf{Sensor Data Integration:} Identification of existing standards and protocols for exporting medically relevant data and integrating real-time sensor data from medical devices and wearables into Hospital Information Systems (HIS).
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Eligibility criteria}
Papers were included if they met the criteria for their specific domain:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Synthetic Data:} Papers were included if they utilized LLMs, Generative AI, or model-based approaches employing state machines specifically for the purpose of generating synthetic clinical health data.
\item \textbf{Sensor Data:} The papers had to include the collection of sensor data and their integration in a HIS or the Electronic Health Records (EHR).
\end{enumerate}
Papers were excluded when they only collected sensor data to train machine learning algorithms without the integration into the HIS or EHR.

\subsubsection*{Information sources}
Literature for this research project was systematically retrieved from two primary electronic databases: PubMed and the IEEE Xplore Digital Library. These sources were selected to ensure a comprehensive coverage of both the clinical and technical dimensions of the research topics. The search was conducted during March 2026.

\subsubsection*{Search Strategy}
The literature retrieval was conducted using two domain-specific search strings. 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Synthetic Data:} For IEEE Xplore and PubMed the following search string was used: ((\enquote{synthetic data generation} OR \enquote{synthetic clinical data} OR \enquote{synthetic data} OR \enquote{synthetic patient} OR \enquote{synthetic patient data} OR \enquote{synthetic EHR} OR \enquote{Synthea})
    \textbf{AND}
    (\enquote{large language models} OR \enquote{LLM} OR \enquote{generative AI} OR \enquote{autonomous agents} OR \enquote{agent-based} OR \enquote{machine learning})
    \textbf{AND}
    (\enquote{clinical workflows} OR \enquote{patient history} OR \enquote{medical records} OR \enquote{case vignettes} OR \enquote{longitudinal} OR \enquote{simulated patients}))
    
    No restrictions were applied regarding publication year or language.
    
    \item \textbf{Sensor Data:} The search string used for PubMed was: ((\enquote{Hospital Information Systems} [MeSH] OR \enquote{Electronic Health Records} [MeSH]) 
    AND (\enquote{sensor*} OR \enquote{sensor data} OR \enquote{wearable*}) 
    AND (\enquote{storage} OR \enquote{archiving} OR \enquote{persistence} OR \enquote{NoSQL} OR \enquote{SQL} OR \enquote{Time-series database} OR \\\enquote{openEHR} OR \enquote{FHIR} OR \enquote{repository})) 
    NOT (\enquote{Blockchain} OR \enquote{Security} OR \enquote{Cryptography} OR \enquote{Privacy Preservation} OR \enquote{Cybersecurity}).
\end{enumerate}
The search string used for IEEE Xplore was identical, only the \textit{[MeSH]} filters were removed. Only articles in German and English were considered, with no restrictions regarding the publication year.

\subsubsection*{Synthesis methods}
Depending on the underlying domain, two different methodological approaches were planned to synthesize the findings.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Synthetic Data:} To evaluate the current landscape of synthetic clinical data generation, a quantitative analysis was designed. Each included paper was systematically reviewed and categorized based on the underlying technology or framework used for data synthesis to identify the primary synthesis engine.
\item \textbf{Sensor Data:} A second quantitative analysis aimed to examine current approaches for integrating sensor data into HIS or EHR systems. For each paper, transmission protocols, utilized interfaces, and communication mechanisms (push or pull) were designated to be extracted and compared.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Definition of Quality Criteria}
To guarantee the pedagogical and technical utility of the synthesized datasets for medical and medical informatics education, a formal set of quality requirements had to be defined. Methodologically, a structured interview guideline was designed to target medical, information technology, and didactic quality metrics. This instrument was utilized to conduct qualitative, target-group-adapted interviews with a cohort of medical experts and specialized IT professionals, with extensive practical experience in academic teaching serving as a unifying selection criterion.

\subsection*{Prototypical Implementation and Synthetic Patient Data Generation}
The practical phase was designed around two parallel implementation workflows, both distinctively engineered to generate synthetic patient data: a rule-based approach utilizing a modified simulation framework and an agentic approach leveraging Large Language Models. This was followed by the design and deployment of an ingestion pipeline targeting the Hospital Information System (HIS).

\subsubsection*{Rule-Based Generation of Synthetic Patient Data via Synthea}
For the rule-based synthesis of patient data, the open-source Synthea repository was utilized. To adapt Synthea’s native North American healthcare and demographic profiles to a European context, the repository was forked to generate localized German synthetic patient datasets, introducing German names, street addresses, cities, hospitals, and health insurance entities.

To prevent an exponential proliferation of virtual clinicians within the synthetic dataset, a provider-pooling strategy was conceptualized to scan the FHIR bundles and systematically substitute Synthea-generated clinicians with a fixed pool of pre-existing HIS users assigned to five specialized departments (Cardiology, Neurology, Chest Pain Unit, Stroke Unit, and Neurological Rehabilitation).

\subsubsection*{Large Language Model-Driven Synthetic Patient Data Generation}
In parallel, an agentic data synthesis pipeline was engineered to generate synthetic patient data using Large Language Models. Advanced prompts were developed for the Gemini 3.1 Pro (High) model to synthesize structured clinical records directly as valid FHIR bundles. To mitigate clinical hallucinations and ensure realistic disease trajectories within the synthetic patient profiles, the model was supplied with a restricted, reference taxonomy of valid diagnoses and medications.

To resolve formatting inconsistencies within the generated JSON-based FHIR bundles, the prompt architecture was extended with explicit OpenMRS-compatibility criteria and strict structural formatting rules. Additionally, the comprehensive CIEL concept dictionary was integrated into the pipeline layout to handle potential semantic gaps caused by missing concepts in the HIS database.

\subsubsection*{System Integration and HIS Ingestion}
The generated synthetic records were targeted for ingestion into the HIS via its native FHIR API module. To automate laboratory value ingestion, which natively requires a complex two-stage process in Bahmni (order placement and laboratory information system callback), an initial Python script was developed to simulate HTTP network requests captured from UI interactions, utilizing direct SQL database queries as a fallback mechanism for dynamic URL connections.

In parallel, real-world physiological sensor data was prepared for the ingestion pipeline. This empirical dataset was gathered from three human participants who tracked their continuous glucose levels over a duration of two weeks utilizing the FreeStyle Libre 3 sensor. To resolve temporal limitations regarding historical timestamps, a low-level FHIR Observation schema accepted by the HIS was identified to allow seamless imports via the regular FHIR interface.

\subsubsection*{Dynamic Temporal Data Ingestion}
To transform the static database into a \enquote{living} clinical environment, a temporal ingestion pipeline was deployed. A custom Python script was designed to parse the synthesized clinical data into individual, chronological FHIR transactions, persist them within a local SQLite database, and append a target \enquote{Scheduled-Time} attribute to each entry. A secondary script was configured to execute continuously as a cronjob on the central HIS server to query the SQLite database at regular intervals and execute the FHIR import once the scheduled execution time was reached.

\subsection*{Evaluation of the Investigated Approaches}
To objectively measure the efficacy, complexity, and structural quality of the synthesized datasets against real-world clinical baselines, a rigorous evaluation framework was established.

\subsubsection*{Data Cohort Selection and Homogenization Pipeline}
The evaluation matrix was designed to incorporate data from three distinct technological dimensions: rule-based simulation (Synthea), agentic generative models (LLM), and real clinical records derived from anonymized patient records extracted from the public MIMIC-III dataset. A uniform 40/40/40 split was planned to ensure statistical balance.

To eliminate evaluative bias and ensure a strict double-blind testing protocol, a universal PDF-based rendering pipeline was engineered to present all records in an identical, visually indistinguishable format. A two-part script infrastructure was planned: the first script to extract and normalize the heterogeneous source data (Synthea's tabular CSV, JSON-based LLM bundles, and MIMIC-III sources) into an intermediate CSV template, and the second script to parse this template into uniform PDF documents.

To mask demographic anonymity in the real clinical baseline, records were algorithmically populated with pseudo-randomized German identities cross-referenced with the patient's biological sex. To account for variations in information depth, the PDF display layout was strictly homogenized to feature five clinical dimensions: allergies, diagnoses, medications, procedures, and laboratory/vital signs.

\subsubsection*{Evaluation Infrastructure}
Data collection was facilitated by developing a password-protected, web-based application split into a dual-pane interface, where the left pane dynamically renders the randomized patient PDF and the right pane exposes scoring instruments composed of 5-point Likert scales alongside open-ended qualitative feedback fields.

To control for observer bias, three anonymized evaluator profiles were generated and distributed by an external, blinded coordinator. The web application was programmed to enforce data integrity by preventing duplicate evaluations, caching session progress persistently within a cloud database, and automatically filtering out completed records. Standardized execution was maintained via a comprehensive, written evaluation protocol.

Unlike the synthesized patient trajectories, the authentic physiological sensor data streams were designated to be evaluated purely on structural ingestion limits and frontend display possibilities within the Heidelberger studiKIS environment, focusing on the capacity of the infrastructure to handle and render granular high-frequency data without compromising dashboard performance or user-end scannability.
